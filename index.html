Thesis

Computer use is the next frontier.
Agents need somewhere to learn.

Frontier labs are racing to build computer-use agents that can automate the $3 trillion of human labor spent on a computer every year. The models aren't struggling because they're dumb — they haven't had enough practice. Ask Einstein to book a JetBlue flight and he'll figure it out, slowly. Give a ten-year-old three months on an iPad and they'll crush it. Models need muscle memory. Muscle memory needs surface area. That's the gap Frist closes.

01 — Opportunity

A $3 trillion market.

Automating even a fraction of the work people do on a computer is the biggest economic prize in software since the browser. Every lab building a frontier model is pointed at it.

02 — Bottleneck

Labs are environment-constrained.

The bottleneck isn't compute or intelligence — it's realistic environments where agents can train. There's a gap between a model that can use a website on a benchmark and one that can do so in the wild. Environments are how that gap closes.

03 — Surface

Every site. Every app.

Computer use isn't just the browser. Agents need to operate desktop applications, multi-step workflows, and the full surface of a person's workday. The training ground has to cover all of it.

Runs offline

No server, no CAPTCHAs.

Every environment is fully self-contained. No network latency, no rate limits, no anti-bot checks standing between the agent and the lesson.

Deterministic state

Reset, branch, replay.

State is first-class. Any environment can be rewound to a known point, forked for parallel rollouts, and replayed as many times as training requires.

Training speed

Millions of rollouts.

Built from the start for RL. Environments scale horizontally and run at training speed, not human speed — enough surface area for the muscle memory agents need.

The long arc

Computer use is
a permanent layer.

Most software people touch every day doesn't expose an API. Emerging agent-to-app standards are early, partial, and years from covering the long tail — many can't even complete a transaction today. Agents that can operate real interfaces are the backward-compatibility layer for the entire stack, and they'll be needed for a long time. As they get cheaper and more capable, use cases unlock themselves.
